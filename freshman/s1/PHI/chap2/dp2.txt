I do not agree with Wolf, or at least with her whole thesis.
A moral saint is not the ideal form of human existence.
Wolf lists the many flaws that can be found in the moral saint, irregardless of their rational or loving disposition.
They will systematically deny themselves of integral aspects of the human experience in pursuit of their fanatical moralism.
This doesn't lend well to good companionship, and is not what I would normally seek in a partner or friend. 
Although, to view a saint only through this lens would be missing the forest for the trees. 
This hypothetical saint is not defined solely by their faults. 
Every person is a combination of flaws and desirable personality traits.
An average person has flaws on an equal level with the saint, but their desirable personality traits are far outclassed by a saint.
I would want to be friends with a moral saint only out of a selfish motivation.
The support and unconditional love that one would give me far outpaces that of almost any other person.
Essentially while the moral saint would not be a perfect companion and is defiantly not an ideal to strive for personally, they would still be an amazing friend.
